# Documentation as Lab Infrastructure

## The Core Problem

When things go wrong in the lab -- equipment failures, experimental errors -- the knowledge of how to handle them lives in people's heads. Without documentation, each recurrence costs the same time and frustration as the first.[^1] This is compounded when team members are not at their best and lack a written reference to fall back on.[^2]

## Documentation as a Safety Net

The argument for SOPs and incident documentation isn't about blame -- it's about resilience. When decisions are already written down, execution doesn't depend on being sharp in the moment.[^3] This applies to both equipment troubleshooting[^1] and experimental decision-making, like knowing to check negative controls before investing hours in imaging.[^4]

## Personnel and Expectations

Two patterns emerge from recent lab frustrations:

**Emotional state as a blocker.** When asked to document a fix, the response was essentially "I'm too frustrated."[^5] The expectation should be: acknowledge the rationale, and do it.[^6] Future hires need to be can-do people who don't use their emotional state as a reason not to follow through.[^7]

**Inconsistent execution.** Quality work happens sometimes, but it's variable.[^8] When someone can't reliably make good judgment calls, the response is to provide more explicit guidance and documentation -- which shifts burden onto the PI.[^9] This raises a hard question about ROI: is it better to have fewer, highly reliable people (like Maria Payne) than to spread resources across people who need heavy guidance?[^10]

## The Leadership Tension

There's genuine discomfort in evaluating people this way.[^11] But resource constraints force the question: is each person's contribution meeting the requirements of the work?[^12] The pragmatic answer may be to invest in fewer strong executors paired with thorough documentation, rather than more people who need constant direction.
