## Insights

### 1. You are overwhelmingly a Goal/Intention thinker
**Goal/Intention is your single most frequent thought type** — roughly 1 in 5 sentences is about what you want to do, build, or achieve. Combined with Judgment/Assessment (which often evaluates whether a goal is worth pursuing), evaluative and normative content accounts for **38% of all captured thought**. This is the thinking profile of someone who is naturally strategic and action-oriented. You think by deciding what matters and then moving toward it.

### 2. Questions are almost completely absent
Only 4 out of ~135 tagged sentences were questions, and all were exploratory ("where are the times where you want to be fast?"). **Zero factual questions, zero explanatory questions, zero critical questions.** This is the most striking gap. You capture what you want, what you observe, and what you judge — but rarely what you don't know. Either you're not capturing your curiosity, or your curiosity is channeled directly into action (goals) rather than inquiry (questions).

### 3. Contrast/Tension is your signature analytical move
When you do slow down to think analytically, you almost always frame things as productive tensions: speed vs. polish, tool-building vs. output production, being a good boss vs. evaluating ROI, fun vs. duty. **6 of your 10 relational thoughts are Contrast/Tension.** This is an unusually strong analytical instinct — you naturally see the world in dialectics rather than simple choices.

### 4. Concepts and frameworks are extremely underrepresented
Only 1 explicit concept ("maker mentality") and zero frameworks, theories, or schemas were captured across 17 notes. You're clearly building mental models — "capture first, organize later," "documentation as safety net," "sequencing tools vs. output" — but you don't name or externalize them as concepts in the moment. They emerge later during processing rather than during capture.

### 5. Evidence and reasoning are nearly invisible
Only the VHL paper reading (1357) produced empirical evidence captures. No statistical evidence, no counter-evidence, no warrants, no backing. This makes sense for stream-of-consciousness capture, but it means the evidence for your claims lives mostly in your head, not in your notes.

### 6. Your richest captures are narratives you rarely tell
The two personnel narratives (1222, 1224) are by far your longest, most detailed, and most analytically rich captures. They contain observations, judgments, values, principles, conditional knowledge, analogies, and recommendations — packed into a narrative structure. But narratives represent only 2% of your total output. When you storytell, you think deeply. You just don't do it often.

### 7. Metacognition appears as observation, not as a first-class practice
You notice things about your own cognition ("I'm noticing that I get distracted," "I don't know why I'm not wanting to do it") — but these are tagged as Observations, not as deliberate metacognitive reflection. There's no "here's what I learned about how I work" or "here's when my process breaks down."

## Recommendations

### Capture more questions
Your thought profile is heavy on "what I want to do" and light on "what I don't know." Try ending captures with an explicit question: "What I'm wondering is..." or "What I don't understand yet is..." This would feed the permanent note process much richer material and would balance Goal/Intention with genuine inquiry. **Even one question per capture would triple your current rate.**

### Name your concepts when they crystallize
You're building frameworks constantly — you just don't label them. When you notice yourself articulating a principle (like "make time for both" or "the real discipline is sequencing"), pause and name it. Named concepts become reusable across notes. "Maker mentality" was your one explicit concept and it became a linchpin of multiple permanent notes.

### Lean into narrative captures for complex situations
Your two narrative captures (the Jessica and Isabel stories) produced more analytical depth per sentence than any other capture type. When you're processing a complex interpersonal or strategic situation, telling the story from beginning to end seems to unlock your best thinking. Consider making narrative a deliberate capture mode for difficult situations rather than something that happens accidentally.

### Start capturing evidence when reading papers
The VHL capture (1357) was excellent — a specific datum with immediate relevance to your work. But it was the only evidence-type capture in the entire corpus. Your [[Capture First, Organize Later#Paper Reading Workflow|paper reading workflow]] is designed for this. Try capturing at least one datum or finding per paper read, even if it's just a sentence.

### Use your Contrast/Tension instinct deliberately
Since productive tensions are your natural analytical tool, consider making "What tension am I noticing?" an explicit prompt. The [[Capture First, Organize Later]] note was built almost entirely from tension captures. This is your strongest thinking mode — use it on purpose.

### Add a periodic "What did I learn?" reflection
Currently, almost all captures are forward-looking (goals, plans, evaluations of potential). A complementary backward-looking reflection — "What did I learn today?" — would generate more [[Observations & Experiences]], Concepts, and Evidence that the processing step can synthesize. Even a weekly version would substantially diversify your thought profile.
