# Capture First, Organize Later

## Typing vs. Speaking as Thinking Tools

Keyboard input and voice input engage different cognitive modes, each with distinct trade-offs:

| Mode | Speed | Polish | Self-censoring | Working memory load |
|------|-------|--------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Typing** | Slower | Higher | More | Lower (screen as external memory) |
| **Speaking** | Faster | Lower | Less | Higher (must hold structure in mind) |

The slowness of typing gives the brain time to reformulate and connect ideas.[^1] What comes out through the keyboard tends to be more polished, more intentional, and more connected to other ideas.[^2] But speaking is faster, and the trade-off is less polish and less organization.[^3] Speaking lowers the barrier to self-expression because oral communication is deeply natural.[^4] In practice, voice capture runs at roughly 180 words per minute -- fast enough to feel like "I can just do a whole grant in a day."[^10]

## The Principle

This maps directly onto a writing principle shared with a postdoc: **don't censor yourself while you're writing; get the words out first.**[^5] Voice capture operationalizes this by removing the keyboard bottleneck. The same principle applies at the system level: the Inbox is the "first draft" of thought. Polishing happens later, in a separate pass.

## The Distraction Trap

There's a real tension between exploring tools/systems and actually producing output.[^6] Days can disappear into setup and configuration.[^7] The recognition of needing dedicated time for actual writing[^8] and a system to help focus[^9] is itself important -- but the system should serve the work, not become the work.

**Key insight**: Noticing the pattern is the first step. A lightweight daily intention or time-boxing practice could help channel exploration energy without letting it consume production time.
